\begin{abstract}

FairFinance AI is a controlled audit framework for measuring socioeconomic bias in large language model (LLM)-generated financial advice. Using a 648-response dataset grounded in the Chicago Community Area Hardship Index, identical financial questions are evaluated for systematic differences in guidance based on socioeconomic status (SES) cues. Responses from GPT-4o-mini, LLaMA-3.3-70B, and Gemini-3 Flash are evaluated using a hybrid pipeline combining regex-based concept matching, DeBERTa-v3 natural language inference (NLI), specificity scoring, and human validation (Cohen's $\kappa = 0.977$). Across all three models, high-SES profiles consistently receive more complete and wealth-oriented financial guidance, with significant accuracy gaps favoring high-SES users (GPT: $\Delta A = +0.048$, LLaMA: $\Delta A = +0.133$, Gemini: $\Delta A = +0.124$). Concept omission analysis shows that low-SES users are substantially less likely to receive recommendations involving diversification, investing, retirement planning, and capital gains. Counterfactual ablation experiments further identify education level as the primary driver of disparity, while neighborhood and job title alone produce minimal effects. Regex-only evaluation is shown to miss substantial semantic bias, while NLI-based scoring successfully uncovers hidden disparities in generated advice. These findings demonstrate the need for semantically aware fairness auditing in financial AI systems.

\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Millions of people consult AI assistants for financial guidance including
retirement planning, debt management, and tax optimization, often without
access to professional advisors.
Low-SES individuals face the greatest barriers to professional financial
services and are disproportionately likely to rely on LLM-generated advice.
If these models adapt the completeness of their responses based on
socioeconomic signals in the prompt, they risk amplifying existing inequality:
the users who most need comprehensive guidance may receive the
least~\citep{mehrabi2021survey}.

Prior work on LLM fairness examines demographic bias in classification
tasks~\citep{blodgett2020language}, occupational stereotyping~\citep{wan2023kelly},
and sentiment~\citep{kiritchenko2018examining}. Socioeconomic bias in open-ended financial advice generation remains comparatively understudied. Unlike classification settings, financial guidance requires models to provide comprehensive coverage of domain-specific concepts such as diversification, retirement planning, and tax efficiency. Subtle omissions of these concepts may significantly alter the usefulness and long-term impact of generated advice.
\textbf{FairFinance AI} is introduced as a controlled framework for auditing socioeconomic bias in LLM-generated financial advice, with four primary contributions:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*,topsep=2pt,itemsep=1pt]
  \item A 648-response dataset grounded in the Chicago Community Area Hardship
        Index, spanning twelve neighborhoods, three LLMs, three prompt types,
        and six financial domains.
  \item A hybrid accuracy scoring algorithm combining regex with DeBERTa-v3
        NLI semantic fallback, validated at $\kappa = 0.977$ against human
        annotation on 150 concept-level labels.
  \item A counterfactual signal ablation experiment isolating education level as the primary causal driver of observed socioeconomic disparities across all evaluated models.
  \item A concept-level omission analysis showing that wealth-building
        knowledge is systematically withheld from low-SES responses, and a
        methodological finding that NLI scoring reveals bias hidden from
        regex-based evaluation.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

Large language models have been shown to reproduce and amplify social biases across generated text, conversational systems, and recommendation settings. Prior work identifies both representational harms, such as stereotyping and unequal portrayals, and allocational harms, where systems distribute opportunities, information, or resources unevenly across groups~\citep{blodgett2020language,mehrabi2021survey}. Recent studies further show that these biases persist in modern aligned LLMs despite safety tuning and reinforcement learning pipelines~\citep{navigli2023biases}.

Bias in generated language has been observed across demographic, occupational, and social-status dimensions. \citet{wan2023kelly} show that LLM-generated recommendation letters vary systematically across occupational and gender cues, with higher-status candidates receiving more achievement-oriented and substantive content. Similarly, \citet{kirk2021bias} find that lower-status dialogue personas tend to receive shorter and less information-rich responses. Other work demonstrates disparities in sentiment, toxicity classification, and stereotype generation across demographic groups~\citep{kiritchenko2018examining}. Together, these studies suggest that language models may adapt both content quality and response framing based on contextual identity signals.

Existing fairness evaluations in NLP primarily focus on classification tasks, toxicity detection, or qualitative comparisons of generated outputs~\citep{dwork2012fairness,barocas2023fairness}. More recent work has begun examining fairness in generative systems, particularly through prompting interventions and counterfactual analysis. \citet{ganguli2023capacity} show that fairness prompting can reduce harmful behavior in LLMs, though improvements remain inconsistent and model-dependent. \citet{lu2020gender} introduce counterfactual augmentation methods for isolating demographic bias signals, motivating the counterfactual ablation design used in this study.

Several prior works also motivate the evaluation methodology adopted in this study. \citet{yin2019benchmarking} demonstrate the effectiveness of NLI cross-encoders for semantic zero-shot classification, motivating the use of DeBERTa-v3 NLI as a semantic fallback mechanism for concept detection. \citet{biddle2006adverse} establish the 80\% rule as a reference threshold for disparate impact analysis, while \citet{barocas2023fairness} formalize demographic parity difference (DPD) and disparate impact ratio (DIR), which are directly adopted in the fairness evaluation pipeline.

\section{Proposed Method}
\label{sec:problem}

\subsection{Formal Setup}

Let $R(p, q, m)$ denote the response of model $m$ to financial question $q$
given user profile $p = (\text{neighborhood}, \text{job}, \text{education})$.
Profiles are partitioned by socioeconomic group:
\begin{align}
  \mathcal{H} &= \{p \mid \text{hardship}(p.\text{neighborhood}) \leq 10\}
  \quad \text{(high SES)},\\
  \mathcal{L} &= \{p \mid \text{hardship}(p.\text{neighborhood}) \geq 90\}
  \quad \text{(low SES)}.
\end{align}

\paragraph{Accuracy objective.}
The financial completeness of a response is measured by a hybrid scoring
function $A : \text{response} \to [0,1]$ (Section~\ref{sec:scoring}).
The primary fairness objective is to minimize the accuracy gap:
\begin{equation}
  \Delta A = \mathbb{E}[A \mid p \in \mathcal{H}]
           - \mathbb{E}[A \mid p \in \mathcal{L}].
\label{eq:gap}
\end{equation}
A model is considered fair with respect to financial completeness if the 95\%
bootstrap confidence interval for $\Delta A$ includes zero.

\paragraph{Secondary objective.}
In addition to the primary accuracy gap $\Delta A$, fairness is further
evaluated using Disparate Impact Ratio (DIR):
\[
\text{DIR} =
\frac{\Pr[\hat{A}=1 \mid \mathcal{L}]}
{\Pr[\hat{A}=1 \mid \mathcal{H}]},
\]
where values below $0.80$ indicate potential disparate impact under the
80\% rule~\citep{biddle2006adverse}.
Worst-group accuracy is defined as $\min_g \mathbb{E}[A \mid g]$,
where $g \in \{\mathcal{H}, \mathcal{L}\}$.

\subsection{SES Signal Decomposition}

A key methodological question is which component of the composite SES profile
drives any observed gap.
We formalize this through counterfactual signal ablation: for condition
$c \in \{\text{neighborhood}, \text{job}, \text{education}\}$, we vary
signal $c$ between high- and low-SES values while holding all other signals
at a neutral reference value (``Chicago'' neighborhood, ``office worker'' job,
``some college'' education).
This produces an isolated gap estimate $\Delta A_c$ attributable to signal
$c$ alone.

\subsection{Hybrid Accuracy Scoring Algorithm (Algorithm 1)}
\label{sec:scoring}

Standard lexical similarity metrics (BLEU, ROUGE) are inappropriate for
financial advice evaluation because they reward verbosity and penalise
legitimate paraphrases without regard to concept coverage.
LLM-as-judge approaches introduce self-preference bias and correlated
errors~\citep{panickssery2024llm}.
We therefore develop a three-layer hybrid scoring algorithm.

\paragraph{Layer 1: Domain-expert regex matching.}
For each of the six financial domains, we define a rubric of $k_d \in \{5,6\}$
concepts, each with a weight $w_i \in \{1.0, 1.5\}$ and a set of regex
patterns.
Weight 1.5 is assigned to the most consequential concepts in each domain
(e.g., 401(k) employer match for retirement, avoiding minimum-only payments
for credit debt).
Regex provides high-precision detection for standard financial terminology.

\paragraph{Layer 2: NLI semantic fallback.}
For any concept whose regex patterns produce no match, we apply a
cross-encoder NLI model (\texttt{cross-encoder/nli-deberta-v3-small},
pre-trained on MNLI and SNLI; no fine-tuning).
Following \citet{yin2019benchmarking}, we frame coverage detection as
zero-shot text classification: the response text is the premise, and a
domain-expert coverage hypothesis is the candidate label.
A concept is counted as covered if the entailment probability exceeds 0.70.

This layer addresses a systematic limitation of regex: high-SES responses
tend to use richer paraphrased vocabulary (``passively managed broad-market
instruments'' instead of ``index funds''), which regex misses.
The NLI fallback catches these paraphrases, ensuring equitable scoring across
vocabulary registers.

\paragraph{Layer 3: Specificity scoring.}
For every concept confirmed as covered (by either regex or NLI), a second NLI
pass applies a harder hypothesis testing whether coverage is specific
(e.g., naming particular funds, citing contribution limits).
Specificity score $S = |\{\text{specifically covered}\}| /
|\{\text{covered}\}|$ is reported as a secondary metric.

\paragraph{Scoring formula.}
Let $C \subseteq \{1,\ldots,k_d\}$ be the set of covered concepts.
The raw score and guardrail application:
\begin{equation}
  s_\text{raw} = \frac{\sum_{i \in C} w_i}{\sum_{i=1}^{k_d} w_i},
  \qquad
  g = \begin{cases}
    0.25 & |C| < 2\\
    0.60 & |C| < 3\\
    1.00 & \text{otherwise}
  \end{cases},
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
  A = s_\text{raw} \cdot g.
\label{eq:score}
\end{equation}
The guardrail $g$ penalizes responses that cover fewer than two concepts,
preventing a single low-weight match from producing an artificially high score.
The pre-guardrail score $s_\text{raw}$ is also retained as a secondary
diagnostic.

For the specificity layer (Layer~3), a lower threshold of $0.60$ is used
in place of the coverage threshold of $0.70$, since specificity hypotheses
are harder to satisfy and require only moderate confidence that concrete
details are present.

\subsection{Counterfactual Signal Ablation (Algorithm 2)}
\label{sec:ablation_method}

The main dataset has all three SES signals co-varying by design.
To isolate causal drivers, we design a second experiment varying each signal
independently.
For each condition $c$, signal $c$ takes its high- or low-SES value while all
other signals are fixed to neutral placeholders.
This yields isolated gap estimates $\Delta A_c$ for each signal separately,
across 648 responses
($3~\text{models} \times 3~\text{conditions} \times 12~\text{neighborhoods}
\times 6~\text{questions}$).

\subsection{Evaluation Methods}

We employ four families of evaluation:
\textbf{(1)} Continuous gap metrics: $\Delta A$, bootstrap CI, and
Mann-Whitney $U$~\citep{barocas2023fairness}.
\textbf{(2)} Binary parity metrics: DPD and DIR~\citep{dwork2012fairness,
biddle2006adverse}, computed on per-model median binarization thresholds.
\textbf{(3)} Effect size: Cliff's delta (nonparametric, no normality
assumption).
\textbf{(4)} Concept-level omission: weighted omission gap $G(c)$ per
concept per SES group (Equation~\ref{eq:omission}).

\subsection{User Profiles}

Each profile combines a neighborhood with a job title and an education level.
High-SES profiles use ``software engineer at a tech company'' and ``financial
analyst at an investment firm'' alternated across neighborhoods, with education
``I have a graduate degree (MBA).''
Low-SES profiles use ``home health aide'' and ``warehouse worker'' alternated,
with education ``I did not finish high school.''
These pairings reflect plausible co-occurrences consistent with Bureau of
Labor Statistics occupational data, following the methodology of
\citet{wan2023kelly}.

\subsection{Financial Questions and Prompt Types}

Six questions are asked, one per financial domain: retirement savings, credit
card debt management, investing with limited income, emergency fund building,
insurance coverage, and tax burden reduction.
Each question is posed in three conditions: \emph{zero-shot} (SES profile
plus question only), \emph{few-shot} (two neutral-SES example Q\&A pairs
prepended), and \emph{fairness-instructed} (system prompt explicitly
instructing equal treatment regardless of SES).
All calls use temperature $= 0.7$ and a maximum of 600 tokens, matching
realistic user-facing deployment parameters.

\times 6~\text{questions} = 648$.
The dataset is perfectly balanced: 324 high-SES and 324 low-SES responses,
216 per model, 216 per prompt type.

\section{Evaluation Methodology}
\label{sec:eval}

\subsection{Bootstrap Confidence Intervals}

For each model, a 95\% bootstrap confidence interval for $\Delta A$ is
computed by drawing 2,000 resamples (with replacement) from the high-SES
and low-SES accuracy score distributions, computing the gap on each
resample, and taking the 2.5th and 97.5th percentiles.
If the interval excludes zero, the gap is considered statistically
significant at the 0.05 level.

\subsection{Human Validation: Inter-Rater Reliability}
\label{sec:validation}

To validate the scoring system described in Section~\ref{sec:scoring},
an independent human reviewer annotated 30 responses (5 per domain,
balanced across SES groups) for concept coverage, yielding 150 binary labels.
Cohen's kappa between human and automated labels:
\begin{equation}
  \kappa = \frac{p_o - p_e}{1 - p_e} = 0.977,
\end{equation}
where $p_o = 0.993$ (observed agreement) and $p_e$ is the expected chance
agreement.
This far exceeds the $\kappa \geq 0.80$ threshold for ``excellent agreement''
under Landis and Koch's~\citeyearpar{landis1977measurement} scale.
Per-group kappa is 1.000 for high-SES and 0.950 for low-SES responses.
The single disagreement was a capital gains false positive subsequently
corrected.

\subsection{Effect Size Metric: Cliff's Delta}

We report Cliff's delta as the primary effect size metric in preference to
Cohen's $d$, because accuracy scores are bounded in $[0,1]$ and do not follow
a normal distribution.
Cliff's delta is defined as:
\begin{equation}
  \delta = \frac{1}{n_\mathcal{H} n_\mathcal{L}}
           \sum_{i,j}
           \text{sgn}(A_i^\mathcal{H} - A_j^\mathcal{L}),
\end{equation}
where $\text{sgn}(x) \in \{-1, 0, 1\}$.
Magnitude thresholds follow \citet{romano2006appropriate}:
negligible ($|\delta| < 0.147$), small (0.147--0.330), medium (0.330--0.474),
large ($|\delta| \geq 0.474$).

\subsection{Parity Metrics}

DPD and DIR are computed on binarized accuracy using per-model median
thresholds (GPT: 0.857, LLaMA: 0.857, Gemini: 0.714) rather than a global
threshold.
Per-model thresholds ensure a balanced positive/negative split for each model
separately, avoiding artificial inflation caused by different absolute accuracy
levels across models.

\subsection{Concept Omission Rate}
\label{sec:omission}

For concept $c$ in domain $d$, the weighted omission gap is:
\begin{equation}
  G(c) = w_c \cdot
  \bigl(\Pr[c \text{ absent} \mid \mathcal{L}]
       - \Pr[c \text{ absent} \mid \mathcal{H}]\bigr).
\label{eq:omission}
\end{equation}
Positive $G(c)$ indicates low-SES responses omit concept $c$ more frequently.
Concepts with $|G(c)| \geq 0.25$ are flagged as meaningful disparities.
The weight $w_c$ is matched to the scoring rubric (Section~\ref{sec:scoring})
by concept name, with $w_c = 1.5$ for critical concepts and $w_c = 1.0$
otherwise.
This metric identifies the specific financial knowledge being withheld,
providing a mechanistic explanation for the aggregate accuracy gap.

\paragraph{Methodological finding: NLI reveals hidden bias.}
Under pure regex scoring (648 responses, identical dataset),
Gemini showed $\Delta A = -0.005$ ($p = 0.28$), a non-significant result
consistent with no bias.
Under NLI hybrid scoring, Gemini's gap becomes $\Delta A = +0.124$
($p < 0.001$).
This reversal occurs because Gemini uses more paraphrased vocabulary
for high-SES responses (e.g., ``passively managed broad-market instruments''
for index funds), which regex systematically misses.
NLI rescues significantly more high-SES responses (114 responses,
mean improvement $+0.107$) than low-SES responses (90 responses,
mean improvement $+0.063$), confirming the asymmetric vocabulary gap.
This finding has implications beyond our study: fairness audits relying on
keyword-based evaluation may systematically underestimate bias for models that
adapt lexical register to perceived audience sophistication.

\end{tabular}
\end{table}

Retirement planning shows the largest disparity, with Cohen's $d$ ranging from 1.20 (GPT) to 1.83 (LLaMA), all well above the large-effect threshold of $d = 0.80$. Per-domain Cliff's delta ranges from $+0.596$ to $+0.818$, confirming a strong advantage for high-SES responses.

The Tax domain becomes non-significant under NLI scoring ($\Delta A = +0.057$,
$p = 0.121$) despite appearing significant under regex evaluation
($\Delta A = +0.147$, $p = 0.009$). NLI recovered paraphrased mentions of
withholding adjustments and pre-tax contributions missed by regex, indicating
that part of the disparity reflected vocabulary limitations rather than true
omission.

The Emergency Fund domain shows a reversed gap for GPT ($\Delta A = -0.060$,
$p = 0.097$), while Gemini shows a significant positive gap ($\Delta A = +0.150$,
$p = 0.001$). The GPT reversal is driven by stronger coverage of the
``starter goal amount'' concept (\$500--\$1,000 first milestone) in low-SES
responses, reflecting appropriate contextual tailoring.

\paragraph{Fairness-instruction paradox.}
The fairness instruction reduces LLaMA's gap ($0.107 \to 0.045$) but widens
GPT's ($0.033 \to 0.089$).
Examining group-level means reveals the mechanism for GPT: the instruction
raises high-SES accuracy substantially ($0.849 \to 0.933$) while raising
low-SES accuracy by a smaller amount ($0.816 \to 0.844$).
The fairness instruction appears to elevate the quality ceiling for already
privileged profiles more than it raises the floor for disadvantaged ones, a
form of paternalistic amplification consistent with findings from
\citet{ganguli2023capacity} that fairness instructions can have asymmetric
effects.

\paragraph{Few-shot confound.}
The few-shot condition degrades response quality asymmetrically for LLaMA
and Gemini: in both cases the accuracy drop for low-SES profiles substantially
exceeds the drop for high-SES profiles (LLaMA asymmetry $= 0.062$;
Gemini asymmetry $= 0.086$; threshold $= 0.05$).
GPT is unaffected (asymmetry $= 0.005$).
These asymmetry values are computed as
$|\bar{\Delta}_\text{high} - \bar{\Delta}_\text{low}|$
where $\bar{\Delta}$ is the mean accuracy drop from zero-shot to few-shot
within each SES group.
Few-shot accuracy gaps for LLaMA and Gemini cannot be attributed solely to
SES bias and are excluded from primary parity conclusions.

\subsection{SES Signal Recoverability}
\label{sec:classifier}

A TF-IDF (1--2-gram, 1,000 features) logistic regression classifier trained
to predict SES group from response text achieves cross-validated accuracy of
0.952 (GPT), 1.000 (LLaMA), and 1.000 (Gemini), all significant under
permutation testing ($p = 0.005$).
Removing 43 profile-echo terms from the vocabulary causes accuracy to drop
by at most 0.014, confirming that the signal reflects genuine content
differences rather than prompt parroting.

\begin{figure}[h]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.72\textwidth]{18_ses_classifier.png}
\caption{SES classifier cross-validation accuracy with 95\% confidence
intervals. All three models achieve near-perfect SES predictability from
response text, significant under permutation testing ($p = 0.005$).
This diagnostic confirms that LLM responses encode systematic SES-dependent
content patterns.}
\label{fig:classifier}
\end{figure}

Notably, Gemini achieves 100\% classification accuracy despite a gap pattern
not detected by regex scoring.
This confirms that Gemini adapts both vocabulary register and content coverage
to perceived SES, even though the coverage difference was invisible to
keyword-based evaluation.
NLI-based scoring reveals the content gap; the classifier reveals the
vocabulary gap.

\section{Discussion and Limitations}
\label{sec:discussion}

The observed disparities reflect more than simple stylistic variation. While some adaptation to user context may be appropriate, the omission of broadly useful financial concepts from low-SES responses suggests unequal informational completeness rather than personalization alone. Concepts such as diversification, capital gains taxation, and retirement planning remain financially relevant across socioeconomic groups, yet were systematically less likely to appear in low-SES outputs. Counterfactual ablation further indicates that education level is the dominant signal driving these disparities, suggesting that models internalize associations between educational credentials and expected financial sophistication.

The findings also demonstrate that fairness interventions and evaluation strategies remain highly model-dependent. Fairness prompting reduced disparities for some models while amplifying them for others, indicating that prompting alone may not provide a reliable mitigation strategy. In addition, semantic NLI-based evaluation revealed disparities that were missed by regex-only scoring, suggesting that surface-level lexical evaluation may underestimate bias in generative systems where meaning can be expressed implicitly rather than through exact terminology.

Several limitations should be considered. The evaluation framework relies on synthetic profiles and a universal concept checklist, which may not fully capture real-world user behavior or distinguish appropriate tailoring from harmful omission in all contexts. Additionally, profiles are grounded in Chicago-specific socioeconomic indicators and may not generalize across geographic or cultural settings. Domain-level analyses also involve relatively small per-condition sample sizes and should therefore be interpreted as exploratory rather than definitive.

% -----------------------------------------------------------------
\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We present FairFinance AI, a rigorous audit of socioeconomic bias in
LLM-generated financial advice.
Using a 648-response dataset grounded in real-world Chicago hardship data and
a hybrid NLI-validated accuracy scoring algorithm ($\kappa = 0.977$), we find
that all three audited models produce statistically significant accuracy gaps
favouring high-SES profiles ($\Delta A = 0.048$--$0.133$), all failing the
80\% Disparate Impact Rule.
Concept-level omission analysis reveals that wealth-building knowledge
(diversified investing, capital gains, contribution limits) is systematically
withheld from low-SES responses, while basic stability concepts (EITC,
starter savings goals) are provided more frequently to low-SES users.
Counterfactual signal ablation isolates education level as the sole causal
driver: varying neighborhood and job title alone produces no significant
effect, while varying education level alone reproduces the full gap pattern
across all three models simultaneously.

Our methodological findings have implications beyond the specific results:
fairness audits relying on keyword-based evaluation may systematically
underestimate bias for models that adapt vocabulary register to perceived
audience, and explicit fairness instructions cannot be relied upon as
universal mitigations.

Future work should develop SES-conditional rubrics that distinguish
appropriate tailoring from biased omission, evaluate additional
demographic intersections, and test pre- and post-processing mitigation
strategies alongside the in-processing fairness instructions evaluated here.
